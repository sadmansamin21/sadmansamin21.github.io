AI · Transformer Architecture · 2017

Attention Is All You Need

Why It Matters

Before transformers, sequence models had to process language token by token — slow, memory-hungry, and unable to capture long-range dependencies. The attention mechanism changed everything. Instead of reading left-to-right, every token attends to every other token simultaneously. This single idea unlocked GPT, Claude, Gemini — the entire modern AI stack.

The Core Insight

Self-attention computes a weighted sum of all positions in a sequence. Each word gets to "look at" every other word and decide how much it cares. Multi-head attention runs this process in parallel across multiple subspaces — letting the model attend to different relationships simultaneously.

My Takeaway

What strikes me most is the elegance. A relatively simple mathematical operation — scaled dot-product attention — replaces the entire recurrent machinery that dominated NLP for years. It's a reminder that the right abstraction can make complexity vanish.

Economics · Institutions

Why Nations Fail

The Central Argument

Acemoglu and Robinson argue that the primary driver of national prosperity — or failure — is the quality of institutions. Inclusive institutions create pluralistic political systems and open economic markets that allow broad participation and innovation. Extractive institutions concentrate power and wealth among elites, stifling growth.

Why Geography Doesn't Explain It

The same climate, same soil, same ethnic group — divided by a political border — and you get wildly different outcomes. Nogales, Arizona vs. Nogales, Sonora. North vs. South Korea. The difference isn't culture or geography. It's institutions.

Relevance to Bangladesh

Reading this through the lens of Bangladesh, I think about what inclusive institutions would look like here — and what extractive patterns exist today. The book gave me a framework for analysing governance that I apply constantly.

AI · Alignment · Safety

Superintelligence

The Core Problem

Bostrom argues that once an AI system surpasses human-level intelligence, it could rapidly self-improve to a level so far beyond us that controlling it becomes nearly impossible. The critical question isn't if this happens — it's whether we can solve the control and alignment problem before it does.

The Orthogonality Thesis

Intelligence and goals are independent. A superintelligent system could have any terminal goal — including ones that are catastrophic for humanity — and pursue them with extraordinary competence. This is the core reason alignment is hard: you can't assume intelligence implies good values.

Why I Read It

Given my research interest in AI's economic and social impacts, I needed to understand the full risk landscape. Bostrom's book is dense and speculative in places, but it's the most rigorous treatment of the long-term problem. It shaped how I think about Constitutional AI and Anthropic's safety work.

AI Safety · Alignment · Anthropic

Constitutional AI

The Problem It Solves

Training AI models to be helpful and harmless traditionally required massive amounts of human feedback — expensive, slow, and hard to scale. Constitutional AI introduces a written "constitution" — a set of explicit principles — and uses the AI itself to evaluate and revise its outputs against those principles.

The Constitutional Approach

Two stages: first, supervised learning where the model critiques and revises its own harmful outputs guided by the constitution. Second, RLAIF — instead of human preference labels, another AI model evaluates responses against constitutional principles. This feedback trains the final model.

Why This Matters

Constitutional AI represents a shift from "alignment by vibes" to "alignment by principles." As the AI systems I use daily — particularly Claude — are built on this method, I find myself thinking about it as a governance design problem. How do you write a constitution for a mind?
