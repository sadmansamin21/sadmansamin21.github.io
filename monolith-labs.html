A student-built library for researchers who can't afford the paywall.

Monolith Labs is a personal open-access research initiative by Sadman Samin, a student at Dhaka College, Bangladesh. The goal is simple: make it easier for students and researchers — especially in South Asia and the Global South — to find rigorous, peer-quality research on sustainable construction and climate-resilient infrastructure without spending hours hunting through paywalled databases.

This library includes both original systematic reviews produced by Monolith Labs (following PRISMA 2020 and random-effects meta-analysis) and curated external research from fully open-access sources — arXiv, PMC, MDPI, Zenodo, Frontiers, and PLOS ONE. Every single link has been verified to reach a freely readable full text. If it's not free, it's not here.

ML-2025-001

Effects of Rice Husk Ash on Concrete Compressive Strength: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis (2000–2025)

Sadman Samin · Dhaka College, Bangladesh · 2025

5–10% RHA = peak strength>15% threshold = degradationI² = 63.4%Up to +15% 28-day CS

Rice Husk Ash (RHA) is a high-silica pozzolanic agricultural by-product generated at scale across South and Southeast Asia. This systematic review synthesises findings from primary experimental studies published between 2000 and 2025, applying PRISMA 2020 screening protocols and a DerSimonian-Laird random-effects framework to extract quantitative thresholds governing RHA performance in Portland cement concrete. The analysis identifies a peak compressive strength efficiency window at 5–10% RHA replacement by cement mass, within which 28-day strength increases of up to 15% above control mixes are achievable under optimised particle size and processing conditions. Beyond a 15% replacement threshold, consistent strength degradation is observed across the pooled dataset, attributable to dilution of the calcium silicate hydrate matrix and insufficient calcium hydroxide availability. Heterogeneity analysis (I² = 63.4%) underscores significant variability introduced by RHA processing method, curing regime, and water-to-binder ratio. Findings are presented as actionable thresholds for mix designers operating in tropical, high-humidity South Asian environments.

ML-2025-002

Systematic Survey of Machine Learning and Deep Learning for Natural Disaster Prediction and Early Warning Systems in South Asia (2020–2025)

Sadman Samin · Dhaka College, Bangladesh · 2025

42 studies synthesisedFlood R² > 0.98Cyclone accuracy 99.16%Landslide AUC 0.93Seismic AUC 0.8737.5% data leakage rate

South Asia is uniquely vulnerable to climate-induced and tectonic hazards due to the convergence of active lithospheric plates, monsoonal precipitation patterns, and dense coastal catchments. Standard deterministic models are frequently limited by severe transboundary data sharing silos, high computational overhead, and sparse in-situ monitoring networks. Over the period 2020–2025, data-driven machine learning and deep learning architectures have emerged as alternative paradigms capable of extracting complex, non-linear relationships from heterogeneous environmental catalogs. This paper presents a systematic survey of 42 peer-reviewed studies published between 2020 and 2025 across four primary hazard domains: floods, cyclones, earthquakes, and landslides. The synthesized findings indicate that while ensemble gradient-boosting architectures achieve robust predictive performance for water level regression (R² > 0.98) under gauged conditions, their performance degrades significantly in ungauged domains (R² = 0.72–0.78). For tropical storm intensity estimation, ConvLSTM networks integrated with spatial feature extractors yield binary classification accuracies up to 99.16% under optimal sampling. Landslide susceptibility mapping, optimized via national geomorphic inventories, yields AUC-ROC metrics up to 0.93. Conversely, seismic prediction remains the most constrained hazard domain, with temporal validation protocols bounding performance at an AUC of 0.87. This survey conducts a validation integrity audit, revealing that 37.5% of evaluated seismic papers reporting accuracies above 95% suffer from data leakage due to flawed random splitting protocols. Finally, the survey outlines key operational barriers — including explainability deficits and transboundary data blockages — and provides a priority roadmap for the development of deployable early warning architectures.

Our Methodology

How original papers are produced.

Every original paper follows a strict, transparent, reproducible methodology. Every step is documented and auditable.

Step 01

PRISMA 2020 Screening

All literature reviews follow the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses 2020 framework. Screening runs across Web of Science, Scopus, and BASE using pre-registered Boolean search strings with documented inclusion and exclusion criteria.

Step 02

Random-Effects Meta-Analysis

Pooled effect sizes are computed using DerSimonian-Laird random-effects models to account for inter-study heterogeneity. I² and τ² statistics are reported transparently alongside Q-test p-values to characterise variability across source populations.

Step 03

Dose-Response Threshold Mapping

Beyond aggregate effects, we isolate critical dosage thresholds — the specific replacement ratios at which performance peaks, plateaus, or degrades. These are the numbers practitioners need for actual mix design decisions in the field.

Step 04

Zenodo Archival & DOI Assignment

Every completed review and dataset is deposited on Zenodo under CC-BY 4.0, receiving a persistent DOI that ensures long-term citability independent of any institutional infrastructure or server uptime.

Step 05

Open Data Deposition

Extraction matrices and statistical scripts are publicly deposited alongside each paper. Any researcher can fork, audit, extend, or fully replicate any component of any Monolith Labs analysis. Transparency is not optional — it is the architecture.

Step 06

External Paper Curation

External papers are selected for methodological rigour, fully verified open-access availability (arXiv, PMC, MDPI, Frontiers, PLOS ONE, Zenodo), and relevance to sustainable infrastructure in South Asia. All links are tested and go directly to freely readable full texts. No paywalled sources are ever listed.
