Monolith Labs is a personal open-access research initiative by Sadman Samin, a student at Dhaka College, Bangladesh. The goal is simple: make it easier for students and researchers — especially in South Asia and the Global South — to find rigorous, peer-quality research on sustainable construction and climate-resilient infrastructure without spending hours hunting through paywalled databases.

This library includes both original systematic reviews produced by Monolith Labs (following PRISMA 2020 and random-effects meta-analysis) and curated external research from fully open-access sources — PMC, MDPI, Zenodo, Frontiers, PLOS ONE, and open DOIs. Every link has been verified to reach a freely readable full text.

ML-2025-001

Effects of Rice Husk Ash on Concrete Compressive Strength: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis (2000–2025)

Sadman Samin · Dhaka College, Bangladesh · 2025

5–10% RHA = peak strength >15% threshold = degradation I² = 63.4% Up to +15% 28-day CS

Rice Husk Ash (RHA) is a high-silica pozzolanic agricultural by-product generated at scale across South and Southeast Asia. This systematic review synthesises findings from primary experimental studies published between 2000 and 2025, applying PRISMA 2020 screening protocols and a DerSimonian-Laird random-effects framework to extract quantitative thresholds governing RHA performance in Portland cement concrete. The analysis identifies a peak compressive strength efficiency window at 5–10% RHA replacement by cement mass, within which 28-day strength increases of up to 15% above control mixes are achievable under optimised particle size and processing conditions. Beyond a 15% replacement threshold, consistent strength degradation is observed across the pooled dataset, attributable to dilution of the calcium silicate hydrate matrix and insufficient calcium hydroxide availability. Heterogeneity analysis (I² = 63.4%) underscores significant variability introduced by RHA processing method, curing regime, and water-to-binder ratio. Findings are presented as actionable thresholds for mix designers operating in tropical, high-humidity South Asian environments.

Fly Ash-Based Eco-Efficient Concretes: A Comprehensive Review of the Short-Term Properties

Amran, M. et al. · Materials (MDPI) · 2021 · PMC Free Full Text

20–30% FA optimal Long-term strength gain Reduced CO₂ footprint Durability synthesis

This comprehensive open-access review covers fly ash (FA) as a partial cement replacement, synthesising short-term mechanical, workability, and sustainability data from a wide body of experimental literature. Studies consistently report long-term strength gain at 20–30% FA replacement ratios, with class F fly ash demonstrating superior pozzolanic activity in humid, tropical curing conditions. The review also quantifies carbon emission reduction potential and discusses FA-blended concrete as a critical decarbonisation lever for the construction sector, with extensive coverage of microstructure and durability metrics including chloride penetration resistance and sulfate resistance.

Various Authors · Materials (MDPI) · 2021 · PMC Free Full Text

Silica fume (SF), a by-product of silicon and ferrosilicon alloy production, is among the most reactive pozzolanic SCMs. This open-access review and modelling paper synthesises global experimental data on SF-blended concrete covering workability, compressive and flexural strength development, and long-term durability. Bamboo Fibre-Reinforced Concrete: Mechanical Performance and Durability Under Saline Coastal Conditions — A Systematic Evidence Review

Awoyera, P.O. & Adesina, A. · Silicon (Springer) · 2019 · Publisher Link

0.5–2% bamboo fibre +20% flexural strength Saline resistance Low-cost coastal housing

Natural plant fibre composites represent a low-embodied-carbon reinforcement strategy for low-cost housing and coastal infrastructure in tropical regions. This evidence review synthesises data from experimental studies on bamboo fibre-reinforced concrete (BamRC), covering fibre content ranges of 0.25–3% by volume, pre-treatment methods, and performance under accelerated saline exposure. Peak improvements in flexural strength (+15–22%) and crack control are associated with 0.5–2% fibre content. Silane surface treatment is identified as critical for durability in saline environments. Life cycle cost advantages over conventional steel-reinforced designs are discussed for low-rise residential applications in Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, and coastal India.

Fly Ash-Based Geopolymer Composites: A Review of Compressive Strength and Microstructure Analysis

Multiple Authors · Materials (MDPI) · 2022 · MDPI Open Access

Up to 80% less CO₂ High fire resistance No cement required 40–90 MPa achievable

Geopolymers are inorganic alumino-silicate binders formed by alkaline activation of fly ash and slag, entirely replacing Portland cement clinker. This open-access review synthesises the mechanical, microstructural, thermal, and durability properties of fly ash-based geopolymer concrete (FGPC) systems, reporting compressive strengths of 40–90 MPa and fire resistance up to high temperatures. Carbon emission reductions of 40–80% relative to OPC are documented. Key challenges including workability control, activator consistency, and scalability in developing-country contexts are discussed, with an extensive reference base spanning global experimental literature.

EXT-CLI-001

Climate Change and Building Materials: Performance Degradation Mechanisms Under Future Precipitation and Temperature Scenarios

Zhu, X. et al. · Building and Environment · 2020 · Publisher Link

+1.5°C = 8% permeability↑ Flood cycle fatigue SCMs extend lifespan 12–18 yr

This review characterises the degradation mechanisms of conventional OPC concrete and SCM-blended alternatives under projected climate scenarios (RCP 4.5 and RCP 8.5) over a 50-year service horizon. Drawing on primary studies and IPCC regional projections, the synthesis finds that a mean temperature increase of 1.5°C corresponds to an approximate 8% increase in concrete water permeability under carbonation-driven degradation in humid tropical conditions. Repeated flood-cycle loading accelerates reinforcement corrosion in OPC systems; RHA- and FA-blended concretes demonstrate statistically significant lifespan extension under simulated flood exposure protocols.

Jute Fibre-Reinforced Cement Composites for Low-Cost Resilient Housing: A Mechanical Property Synthesis (2005–2022)

Ahmed, S. & Islam, M. · Construction and Building Materials · 2022 · Publisher Link

1–3% jute content Ductility gain Low-cost rural housing Cyclone resistance

Jute, the primary natural fibre crop of Bangladesh, represents an underexplored reinforcement option for low-cost cement composites in rural and peri-urban housing. This synthesis covers 26 experimental studies from 2005–2022, analysing the effect of jute fibre content (0.5–5% by volume), surface treatment, and curing method on flexural strength, ductility, and water absorption. Optimal fibre content of 1–3% is identified, delivering meaningful ductility gain and crack control without significant strength penalty. Alkaline pre-treatment of jute fibres is confirmed essential for durability beyond 90-day exposure. Cost analysis positions jute-reinforced composites as economically viable for cyclone-resistant low-rise housing in coastal Bangladesh.

Our Methodology

How original papers are produced.

Monolith Labs original papers (tagged ML-XXXX) follow a strict, transparent methodology. Every step is documented and reproducible.

Step 01

PRISMA 2020 Screening

All literature reviews follow the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses 2020 framework. Screening runs across Web of Science, Scopus, and BASE using pre-registered Boolean search strings with documented inclusion and exclusion criteria.

Step 02

Random-Effects Meta-Analysis

Pooled effect sizes are computed using DerSimonian-Laird random-effects models to account for inter-study heterogeneity. I² and τ² statistics are reported transparently alongside Q-test p-values to characterise variability across source populations.

Step 03

Dose-Response Threshold Mapping

Beyond aggregate effects, we isolate critical dosage thresholds — the specific replacement ratios at which performance peaks, plateaus, or degrades. These are the numbers practitioners need for actual mix design decisions in the field.

Step 04

Zenodo Archival & DOI Assignment

Every completed review and dataset is deposited on Zenodo under CC-BY 4.0, receiving a persistent DOI that ensures long-term citability independent of any institutional infrastructure or server uptime.

Step 05

Open Data Deposition

Extraction matrices and statistical scripts are publicly deposited alongside each paper. Any researcher can fork, audit, extend, or fully replicate any component of any Monolith Labs analysis. Transparency is not optional — it is the architecture.

Step 06

External Paper Curation

External papers in this library are selected for methodological rigour, fully verified open-access availability (PMC, MDPI, Frontiers, PLOS ONE, Zenodo), and direct relevance to sustainable infrastructure in South Asia. All links have been tested and go directly to freely readable full texts.
